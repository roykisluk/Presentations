# Lagos 2006: A Model of TFP

## 1. Research Question
How do labor-market policies affect the aggregate production function in general, and the level of measured total factor productivity (TFP) in particular?

## 2. Audience
Macroeconomists, labor economists, and researchers studying cross-country productivity and growth (e.g., explaining differences in TFP levels due to institutions).

## 3. Method
The paper constructs an aggregative model of TFP in the spirit of Houthakker (1955-1956). It derives an aggregate production function by aggregating across active micro-production units within a frictional labor market. The model builds on the Mortensen and Pissarides (1994) framework with idiosyncratic match productivity shocks and endogenous job creation and destruction. Policy affects TFP by changing the productivity composition of active units (via the job-destruction decision). The paper distinguishes itself from Houthakker by having the "fixed capacity" constraint (capital) be a choice made prior to search, and dealing with dynamic equilibrium rather than static partial equilibrium.

## 4. Data
The paper is predominantly theoretical but includes a calibration exercise in Section 7. The calibration targets average U.S. labor market facts (e.g., employment spells lasting 2.5 years, a job-finding probability of 0.45 per month, an 11% replacement rate, and an assumed 5% rate of labor hoarding). It relies on existing literature for these stylized facts (e.g., Shimer 2005) rather than directly utilizing a microdata sample.

## 5. Statistical Methods
Continuous-time search and matching model. The cross-sectional productivity distribution of active matches is derived analytically. The model uses a Pareto distribution for idiosyncratic shocks to obtain closed-form solutions for the aggregate production function, which takes a Cobb-Douglas form under specific conditions regarding labor hoarding and capital utilization measurement. The paper also extends this to show that a specific, different shock distribution can aggregate to a Constant Elasticity of Substitution (CES) production function.

## 6. Findings
- A standard search model with fixed-proportions micro-level technologies can generate an aggregate constant-returns Cobb-Douglas production function.
- Labor-market policies that affect job creation and destruction distort the selection of which production units remain active, thereby affecting measured TFP.
- **Employment subsidies** and **firing taxes** make firms more tolerant of low productivity, reducing the job-destruction rate and lowering measured TFP.
- **Hiring subsidies** stimulate job creation, increasing market tightness and the worker's outside option, which raises the reservation productivity and increases measured TFP.
- **Unemployment benefits** increase the worker's reservation wage, raising the job-destruction threshold and thereby increasing measured TFP (along with unemployment).
- **Measurement matters significantly:** The bias in standard growth accounting (which assumes competitive, frictionless markets) depends heavily on how capital and labor are measured. If the accountant does not adjust for utilization (e.g., using total capital stock instead of capital in active firms, or employment instead of hours), the measured TFP residual will be severely distorted by changes in labor-market policies.

## 7. Contributions
Provides a formal microfoundation that links labor-market institutions explicitly to aggregate TFP, showing how two economies with identical technologies and shocks can exhibit different TFP levels solely due to labor-market policies. It bridges the gap between micro-level search frictions and macroeconomic growth accounting. It demonstrates theoretically how standard Cobb-Douglas specifications can emerge from frictional micro-foundations, but warns that the interpretation of the parameters and the TFP residual differs substantially from the frictionless competitive benchmark.

## 8. Replication Feasibility
The theoretical results can be mathematically verified. The quantitative exercise is a calibration designed to illustrate magnitudes rather than a formal estimation. The parameters are fully specified in Section 7 (Table 1), making the illustrative numerical results easily replicable, though it does not rely on a complex replication archive of raw data.
